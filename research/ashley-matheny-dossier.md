# Ashley M. Matheny - Research Dossier

## Overview
Ashley M. Matheny is an Assistant Professor of Ecohydrology at the University of Texas at Austin's Jackson School of Geosciences. Her research focuses on understanding how vegetation influences the movement of water between soil and atmosphere, with implications for climate modeling, drought prediction, and forest management.

## Early Life and Education
- **Birthplace**: Small town along the Ohio River in West Virginia
- **Early influences**: Water skiing, canoeing, camping fostered passion for nature
- **BS**: Civil Engineering, The Ohio State University
- **PhD**: Civil, Environmental, and Geodetic Engineering, The Ohio State University (2013-2016)
  - Advisor: Gil Bohrer
  - Dissertation: "Development of a Novel Plant-Hydrodynamic Approach for Modeling of Forest Transpiration During Drought and Disturbance"
- **Postdoc**: Ohio State University (2016-2017)

## Career Timeline
- **2010**: Worked for U.S. Army Corps of Engineers studying water interactions with dams
- **2010**: Witnessed hydraulic jump on Ohio River - pivotal moment redirecting career toward hydrology
- **2013-2016**: PhD research on tree water use and hydraulic strategies
- **2016-2017**: Postdoctoral researcher at Ohio State
- **2017-present**: Assistant Professor at UT Austin Jackson School of Geosciences

## Research Focus
### Core Areas
1. **Ecohydrology**: How vegetation influences water cycling
2. **Plant Hydraulics**: Water storage and transport in trees
3. **Land-Atmosphere Interactions**: Vegetation's role in climate feedbacks
4. **Drought Response**: How trees manage water stress

### Key Innovations
- Developed novel capacitance sensors to measure tree water content
- Created plant hydrodynamic models to improve land-surface model predictions
- Pioneered methods to distinguish hydraulic strategies across tree species
- Extended plant hydraulics models to include halophytes (salt-tolerant plants)

## Impact and Significance
Matheny's work bridges fundamental plant physiology with large-scale climate modeling. Her research has:
- Improved representation of vegetation in Earth system models
- Provided tools for predicting drought impacts on forests
- Advanced understanding of tree mortality mechanisms
- Contributed to forest management strategies under climate change
